Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) Sum Rules

QCD Sum Rules, a pivotal method in theoretical physics, was developed to bridge the gap between observable properties of composite particles, or hadrons, and the fundamental Quantum Chromodynamics theory.
This theory governs the strong interactions between quarks and gluons.

At the heart of this method lies the Operator Product Expansion (OPE) and the concept of quark-hadron duality.
The OPE allows for the expression of the product of two quantum field operators as a sum over local operators.
This sum, which expands in powers of quark masses and the vacuum expectation values of the QCD operators (condensates), contributes to the theoretical side of the sum rules.

These rules have been used to shed light on various properties of hadrons, such as masses, decay constants, and form factors.
They remain an essential tool for probing the low-energy domain of QCD, where the strong coupling constant is substantial and standard methods fall short.

Investigation of the Coupling Constants of Mesons by Using QCD Sum Rules:

In this research, the coupling constants of pseudoscalar and vector mesons are investigated in the framework of QCD Sum Rules at vacuum and at finite temperature.
Theoretical studies on hadronic behaviours at vacuum and at finite temperature and density have a crucial role to understand experimental results and to guide future experiments.

Electronic Structure Theory in Organic Battery Electrode Design

Electronic Structure Theory serves as a critical tool in the realm of organic battery electrode design.
It enables the exploration of electron properties in materials, which is pivotal in understanding their conductivity, reactivity, and structural stability.

Density Functional Theory (DFT), a common method used in these investigations, provides key insights into the electronic structure of many-body systems.
The advantage of DFT is that it focuses on electron density, making the computation easier and more effective for larger systems.

In designing organic battery electrodes, Electronic Structure Theory helps in screening potential materials, predicting their behavior in the battery, and suggesting structural modifications to improve their performance.
Organic materials offer the promise of sustainability, high energy density, and the versatility of organic compounds.

The complexity lies in understanding and optimizing the redox reactions these materials undergo and ensuring their stability under operating conditions.
Here, Electronic Structure Theory becomes invaluable, offering critical insights that aid in designing more efficient, safer, and more environmentally friendly batteries.

My research focused on the design of organic radical battery electrodes using semiconductive polymer structures.
The electronic structures of various polymers were analyzed, with calculations contributing to the understanding of singly occupied molecular orbitals and the potential for polyacetylene to be used as a polymer backbone in organic radical polymers.
